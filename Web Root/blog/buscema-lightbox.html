John Buscema Lightbox Method

How an industry legend produced seven pages of breakdowns per day

David Marshall March 24, 2024

FOO_TOP_INTRO

OR_FOO_TOP_PARAGRAPH

John Buscema (1927-2002) was an American comic book artist best known his 1966-1999 Marvel Comics period. His signature style expressed Jack Kirby's dynamics with Hal Foster's anatomy. After driving Kirby to DC Comics, Marvel made Buscema its go-to artist for virtually every superhero title (Fantastic Four, Silver Surfer, Thor, The Avengers). He quickly became a fan favorite, but never considered drawing superhero comics as anything more than a day job (his work on Conan the Barbarian was a lot more inspired). Buscema's also famous for drawing art he really liked as practice on the back of his uninspired superhero pages.

Marvel's creative process at the time, informally known as the Marvel Method, had the artist plot the story while drawing. Writers then created dialog and captions upon seeing the art. Some artists (Kirby, Wally Wood, Steve Ditko) felt this was wage theft since they weren't paid or credited for co-writing. Others (Buscema, Gene Colan, John Romita) saw it as liberation from working with formal scripts.

Artists like Wood, Al Williamson, and Neal Adams struggled with adapting their elaborate styles to the Kirby-inspired pace of three pages a day. Far as I know, only Buscema, Colan, and Romita successfully transitioned to the Marvel Method.

Buscema's first step was making rough preliminary page breakdowns with graphite on cheap newsprint at original 10-by-15 inch art size, strategically leaving space for word balloons. He then traced these roughs onto professional paper with a lightbox. His "just outlines" were breakdowns for inkers to do finished art. Buscema made most of his Marvel money by producing seven pages of breakdowns a day (two whole books a week). His finished pencils were fully shaded for traditional inking.

Other established artists reject this technique, preferring to do all the preliminary work in thumbnails and pencil on bristol paper. I personally think it's a good way to produce pencil art without a ton of erasing and confusion. Hopefully this sheds some light to its virtues.
